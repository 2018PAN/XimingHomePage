## Background

This project was born out of the **2025 Design Challenge (#DC11)**, an intense international cartography hackathon hosted collaboratively by the Technical University of Wien, the UW Cartography Lab, and the International Cartographic Association (ICA). 

The core objective: **to collaboratively design and produce a comprehensive atlas of the United Nations Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) in less than one week.** Adopted in 2015, the UN's 2030 Agenda includes 17 overarching goals and 231 unique indicators. Our mission was to translate these massive, complex global datasets into visual narratives capable of informing policy and raising public awareness.

## My Contribution

Among the many SDGs assigned during the challenge, my team was tasked with diving deep into **SDG 5: Achieve gender equality and empower all women and girls.** Within this massive framework, we chose a specific, highly impactful angle: **the Digital Gender Gap in mobile phone and internet usage.**

In the information age, unequal access to digital resources translates directly to systemic disadvantages in education, economic independence, and social voice.

## Story Behind the Map

Our map tells a straightforward story: **women’s phone ownership isn’t the same everywhere.** To make this digital divide visible and urgent, I implemented several intentional cartographic strategies:

* **A Geographic Focus on Inequality:** I utilized a custom-projected globe centered on North and Central Africa—the "lightest spots" on our map. By zooming in here, we highlight regions where fewer than half of women own a phone, shifting the viewer's attention to where the gap is most acute.
* **Visualizing the Spectrum:** We used a high-contrast **blue choropleth scheme** on a dark background to create a serious, academic tone. Dark blue represents places where women and men have phones at nearly the same rate, while pale blue marks where women fall far behind. This visual hierarchy ensures that the extremes immediately capture the reader's attention.
* **Moving from "What" to "Why":** A great map doesn't just display numbers; it uncovers systemic barriers. I designed targeted infographics to show that this gap isn't just a statistic: a basic handset can cost up to **25% of a woman’s income**, digital literacy training is hard to come by, and cultural norms often keep women offline. 

Without a phone, women miss out on news, work opportunities, and a chance to join community conversations. Our take-home message is clear: **fixing this digital divide can’t wait.** We need cheaper devices, hands-on training in local communities, and policies that support women’s access to technology. Only then can we close the gender gap and foster meaningful, lasting progress.
